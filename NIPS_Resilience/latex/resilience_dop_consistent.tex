\title{Metastability of Retrieval in LSR Dense Associative Memory}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Dense Associative Memory models with the log-sum-ReLU (LSR) energy function achieve exponential storage ($M = e^{\alpha N}$ patterns) and, below a critical load $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$, are predicted to retrieve perfectly at any temperature.  We show that within this retrieval region, the system is metastable: thermal fluctuations drive escape from the retrieval basin to two-pattern centroids via a Kramers process over a purely entropic barrier.  The escape time scales as $\tau \sim N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ with $\Delta F/T \propto N$, confirmed by Monte Carlo measurements across three decades in $\tau$ ($R^2 = 0.95$).  After the first escape, the system performs a random walk on the pattern overlap graph, hopping between centroids at comparable energy; no true paramagnetic state exists.  The apparent phase boundary observed in simulations is a kinetic crossover ($\tau \approx T_{\mathrm{MC}}$), not a thermodynamic phase transition.  Since $\tau$ grows exponentially with $N$, the retrieval state is effectively stable for practical system sizes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Associative memory networks store patterns so that the correct memory can be retrieved from a noisy or partial cue.  In the classical Hopfield network~\citep{Hopfield1982}, storage capacity scales linearly with the number of neurons~\citep{amit1987statistical}.  Dense Associative Memory (DAM) models replace pairwise interactions with highly nonlinear energy functions, achieving exponential storage: $M = e^{\alpha N}$ patterns for $N$ continuous neurons on the unit sphere, where $\alpha = \ln M/N$ is the logarithmic load per neuron~\citep{demircigil2017model,ramsauer2021hopfield}.

Two kernel-based energy functions have been studied in this setting.  The log-sum-exp (LSE) energy, based on a Gaussian similarity kernel, was analyzed by~\citet{ramsauer2021hopfield}, who proved a zero-temperature capacity $\alpha_c(0) = 0.5$.  More recently, \citet{hoover2025dense} introduced the log-sum-ReLU (LSR) energy for binary neurons, based on the compactly supported Epanechnikov kernel.  For continuous neurons on the $N$-sphere ($|\bx|^2 = N$), the LSR energy takes the form
\begin{equation}
E(\bx) = -\frac{N}{b}\ln\left(\sum_{\mu=1}^{M}\max\!\Big(0,\;1 - b + b\,\phi_\mu\Big)\right),
\qquad \phi_\mu \equiv \frac{\bxi^\mu \cdot \bx}{N}\,,
\label{eq:lsr}
\end{equation}
where $b > 1$ is the rescaled inverse variance controlling the kernel sharpness, $\bxi^\mu$ are the stored patterns on $S^{N-1}(\!\sqrt{N})$, and $\phi_\mu$ is the alignment of the state $\bx$ with pattern~$\mu$.  The compact support introduces a hard wall at $\varphi_c = (b-1)/b$: pattern~$\mu$ contributes to the energy only when $\phi_\mu > \varphi_c$.  This is qualitatively different from the LSE kernel, where all patterns contribute everywhere on the sphere.

The thermodynamic phase boundaries $\alpha_c(T)$ for both kernels on the $N$-sphere were derived by~\citet{icml2026theory} using the replica method, and validated by Monte Carlo (MC) simulations in~\citet{iclr2026mc}.  A key result for the LSR kernel is the existence of a support threshold
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\mathrm{th}} = \frac{\varphi_c^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\!\left(\frac{b-1}{b}\right)^{\!2},
\label{eq:alpha_th}
\end{equation}
below which no spurious pattern falls within the support and retrieval is perfect at any temperature~\citep{icml2026theory}.  For $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$ (the value used throughout this paper): $\varphi_c \approx 0.707$ and $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}} = 0.25$.  This sub-threshold regime has no analog in the LSE model, where a critical line exists at all loads $\alpha > 0$.

The derivation of~\eqref{eq:alpha_th} relies on approximating the alignment distribution on $S^{N-1}$ by a Gaussian $f(\phi) \propto e^{-N\phi^2/2}$, as in~\citet{ramsauer2021hopfield}.  The exact density on the sphere,
\begin{equation}
f(\phi) = C_N\,(1 - \phi^2)^{(N-3)/2}\,,\qquad
C_N = \frac{\Gamma(N/2)}{\Gamma\!\big((N\!-\!1)/2\big)\,\sqrt{\pi}}\,,
\label{eq:exact_density}
\end{equation}
has the same mean ($0$) and variance ($1/N$) but lighter tails: it vanishes at $|\phi| = 1$, whereas the Gaussian assigns nonzero weight there.  Since the LSR noise floor depends on whether any pattern exceeds $\varphi_c$, the lighter tails of~\eqref{eq:exact_density} predict fewer patterns above threshold and shift $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$ to a higher value.  Specifically, the maximum inter-pattern overlap $\varphi_{1,\max}$ (defined by $n(\varphi_{1,\max}) = 1$, i.e.\ on average one pattern reaches this overlap) is $\sqrt{2\alpha}$ under the Gaussian and $\sqrt{1-e^{-2\alpha}}$ under the exact density.  Setting $\varphi_{1,\max}^{\mathrm{exact}} = \varphi_c$ gives $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}^{\mathrm{exact}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1-\varphi_c^2) \approx 0.347$ for $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$.  Finite-$N$ corrections shift this further right ($\approx 0.39$ at $N = 50$).

\noindent However, the MC simulations of~\citet{iclr2026mc} place the LSR retrieval boundary much closer to $\alpha \approx 0.25$ than to~$0.347$.  The Gaussian approximation, despite its unphysical tails, describes the onset of interference more accurately than the exact density~\eqref{eq:exact_density}.  The reason for this is not understood; we note only that finite-$N$ corrections to the exact prediction push $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$ further from the MC value, not closer (see the Remark above).

In this paper we set aside the question of the precise location of $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$ and instead examine what happens \emph{inside} the retrieval region $\alpha < \alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$, where the thermodynamic theory of~\citet{icml2026theory} predicts perfect retrieval at any temperature.  We reproduce and extend the MC simulations of~\citet{iclr2026mc} for the LSR kernel, recording per-trial observables (two replicas $\times$ up to 256 independent disorder samples per $(\alpha, T)$ point) at finite system sizes $N \approx 20$--$50$.

The per-trial overlap distributions reveal that the retrieval region is richer than the thermodynamic prediction suggests.  Figure~\ref{fig:cdf} (left) shows the empirical CDF of the time-averaged alignment $\varphi$ with the target pattern at fixed $T = 0.325$, for several values of~$\alpha$.  At $\alpha = 0.10$, all trials concentrate near $\varphi \approx 0.90$, the Boltzmann equilibrium $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}(T)$ from the self-consistency equation of~\citet{icml2026theory}, not unity, since thermal fluctuations at $T > 0$ reduce the time-averaged alignment.  As $\alpha$ increases toward $0.25$, secondary steps appear at $\varphi \approx 0.83$ and $\varphi \approx 0.48$, indicating that a growing fraction of trials settles into states with lower alignment.  By $\alpha = 0.35$, the primary peak has disappeared.  Figure~\ref{fig:cdf} (right) shows the $T$-dependence at fixed $\alpha = 0.22$: as $T$ increases, the primary peak shifts to lower~$\varphi$ and the secondary steps grow.

These steps at $\varphi \approx 0.83$ and $0.48$ are not predicted by the thermodynamic theory of~\citet{icml2026theory}.  The goal of this paper is to identify the states they correspond to, the mechanism by which the system reaches them from the retrieval basin, the timescales involved, and how all of these depend on $\alpha$, $T$, $N$, and the kernel sharpness~$b$.  The analysis draws comparisons with the classical Hopfield model~\citep{amit1987statistical}, where mixture states and spin-glass phases are known to coexist with retrieval.

\section{Theory of the centroid escape}
\label{sec:theory}

We use the following results from~\citet{icml2026theory} without re-derivation: the internal energy density $u_{\mathrm{LSR}}(\phi) = -b^{-1}\ln[1-b(1-\phi)]$ (ibid., Eq.~14), the geometric entropy $s(\phi) = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1-\phi^2)$ (ibid., Eq.~19), and the single-pattern self-consistency equation for the equilibrium alignment $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}(T)$ (ibid., Eq.~37).  At $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$ and $T = 0.325$: $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} \approx 0.90$.

\subsection{Support caps on the sphere}
\label{ssec:caps}

Each pattern $\bxi^\mu$ defines a \emph{support cap} on the sphere:
\begin{equation}
S_\mu = \big\{\bx \in S^{N-1}(\!\sqrt{N}) : \phi_\mu(\bx) > \varphi_c\big\}\,,
\label{eq:cap}
\end{equation}
the set of states for which pattern~$\mu$ contributes to the LSR energy~\eqref{eq:lsr}.  The cap is centered at $\bxi^\mu$ with angular half-width $\theta_c = \arccos(\varphi_c)$.  Increasing $b$ raises $\varphi_c$ and shrinks the caps.

The \emph{inter-pattern overlap} $\varphi_{1\mu} = \bxi^1\!\cdot\!\bxi^\mu/N$ measures the cosine of the angle between patterns $\bxi^1$ and $\bxi^\mu$.  Two caps $S_1$ and $S_\mu$ intersect if and only if this angle is less than $2\theta_c$, i.e.,
\begin{equation}
\varphi_{1\mu} > \cos(2\theta_c) = 2\varphi_c^2 - 1\,.
\label{eq:cap_intersection}
\end{equation}
The threshold $2\varphi_c^2 - 1$ depends on $b$ through $\varphi_c = (b-1)/b$: it equals~$0$ at $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$, and is positive for larger~$b$.  At $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$, any positive $\varphi_{1\mu}$ suffices, so approximately half of all $M$ patterns have caps intersecting the target's, an exponentially large number $\sim M/2$.  The existence of a geometric intersection does not, however, mean that the system can reach it from the retrieval state.  Reaching the intersection requires a specific rotation in the perpendicular subspace, and the associated free-energy cost restricts the escape-enabling patterns to a much smaller subset $K \ll M/2$.  Among those, the escape is overwhelmingly dominated by the pattern with the largest overlap $\varphi_{1\mu}$, which faces the lowest barrier.

For random patterns on $S^{N-1}(\!\sqrt{N})$, $\varphi_{1\mu}$ is distributed according to the exact density~\eqref{eq:exact_density}, with mean~$0$ and variance~$1/N$.  With $M = e^{\alpha N}$ patterns, the expected number having overlap exceeding any threshold $\varphi^*$ is
\begin{equation}
n(\varphi^*) = M\,\Pr(\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi^*)\,.
\label{eq:n_count}
\end{equation}
The maximum inter-pattern overlap $\varphi_{1,\max}$, defined by $n(\varphi_{1,\max}) = 1$, is $\sqrt{2\alpha}$ in the Gaussian approximation and $\sqrt{1-e^{-2\alpha}}$ using the exact density (see Section~1).

\subsection{The two-pattern centroid}
\label{ssec:centroid}

Consider the target pattern $\bxi^1$ and a spurious pattern $\bxi^\mu$ with inter-pattern overlap $\varphi_{1\mu} > 0$.  We work in the two-dimensional subspace spanned by the orthonormalized pair
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{e}_1 = \frac{\bxi^1}{\sqrt{N}}\,, \qquad
\mathbf{e}_2 = \frac{\bxi^\mu/\sqrt{N} - \varphi_{1\mu}\,\mathbf{e}_1}{\sqrt{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2}}\,.
\label{eq:basis}
\end{equation}
Projecting the state $\bx/\sqrt{N} = a\,\mathbf{e}_1 + v\,\mathbf{e}_2 + \mathbf{c}_\perp$, with $a^2 + v^2 + |\mathbf{c}_\perp|^2 = 1$, the alignments with the two patterns are
\begin{equation}
\phi_1 = a\,, \qquad \phi_\mu = a\,\varphi_{1\mu} + v\sqrt{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2}\,.
\label{eq:overlaps}
\end{equation}

The support conditions $\phi_1 > \varphi_c$ and $\phi_\mu > \varphi_c$ define two half-planes in the $(a,v)$ disk.  Their intersection (the region where both patterns are simultaneously in support) is non-empty whenever $\varphi_{1\mu} > 2\varphi_c^2 - 1$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:cap_intersection}).

Within this intersection, the LSR energy~\eqref{eq:lsr} is
\begin{equation}
\frac{E}{N} = -\frac{1}{b}\ln\!\big[(1-b+b\,\phi_1) + (1-b+b\,\phi_\mu)\big]\,.
\label{eq:E_two}
\end{equation}
From~\eqref{eq:overlaps}, $\phi_1 + \phi_\mu = a\,(1+\varphi_{1\mu}) + v\sqrt{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2}$, which is linear in $(a,v)$ and independent of $\mathbf{c}_\perp$.  Since~\eqref{eq:E_two} is a decreasing function of $\phi_1 + \phi_\mu$, the $T = 0$ energy minimum maximizes this linear form on the sphere $a^2 + v^2 + |\mathbf{c}_\perp|^2 = 1$.  The optimum has $\mathbf{c}_\perp = 0$ (no contribution to the objective) and $(a,v) \propto (1+\varphi_{1\mu},\;\sqrt{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2})$, giving $a = \sqrt{(1+\varphi_{1\mu})/2}$ and $\phi_1 = \phi_\mu = a$.  This defines the \emph{geometric centroid}:
\begin{equation}
\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}^{(0)} = \sqrt{\frac{1+\varphi_{1\mu}}{2}}\,.
\label{eq:centroid}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Centroid energy vs retrieval energy.}
The energy at the geometric centroid (two patterns in support) compared with the retrieval state (one pattern):
\begin{equation}
\frac{E_{\mathrm{ret}}}{N} = u_{\mathrm{LSR}}(\phi_{\mathrm{eq}})\,,\qquad
\frac{E_{\mathrm{cen}}^{(0)}}{N} = -\frac{1}{b}\ln\!\Big(2\big[1-b+b\,\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}^{(0)}\big]\Big)\,.
\label{eq:E_compare}
\end{equation}
At $\alpha = 0.20$, the exact density~\eqref{eq:exact_density} gives $\varphi_{1,\max} = \sqrt{1-e^{-0.4}} \approx 0.57$.  Evaluating at $\varphi_{1\mu} = \varphi_{1,\max}$ (the most-overlapping spurious pattern, which faces the lowest escape barrier and produces the deepest centroid): $\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}^{(0)} = 0.89$, and with $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} = 0.90$: $E_{\mathrm{ret}}/N = 0.12$, $E_{\mathrm{cen}}^{(0)}/N = -0.06$.  The centroid has \emph{lower energy} than the retrieval state.

\paragraph{$k$-pattern centroids.}
If $k$ patterns share a common pairwise overlap $\varphi_{1\mu}$, the symmetric $k$-pattern centroid has overlap $\varphi_k^{(0)} = \sqrt{\varphi_{1\mu} + (1-\varphi_{1\mu})/k}$ and energy $E_k/N = -b^{-1}\ln[k(1-b+b\,\varphi_k^{(0)})]$.  At $\alpha = 0.20$ ($\varphi_{1\mu} = 0.57$): $E_2/N = -0.06$, $E_3/N = -0.10$, $E_4/N = -0.14$.  Multi-pattern centroids are energetically deeper.  However, the escape mechanism of Section~\ref{ssec:escape} does not reach them: at a two-pattern centroid, only $\bxi^1$ and $\bxi^\mu$ are in support, and the Kramers escape rotates the perpendicular component toward a new pattern $\bxi^\nu$.  The sphere constraint $a^2 + v^2 + w^2 + |\mathbf{c}_\perp|^2 = 1$ (where $w$ is the component along $\bxi^\nu$) means that increasing $w$ reduces the budget for $a$ and $v$: generically, one of the two current patterns drops below $\varphi_c$ before $\bxi^\nu$ enters support.  The result is a pair-to-pair transition, not pair-to-triple.

\subsection{The escape path and the Kramers barrier}
\label{ssec:escape}

\paragraph{Stage 1: perpendicular rotation ($\Delta E = 0$).}
At the retrieval state, $a = \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}$ and $v = 0$.  The alignment with $\bxi^\mu$ is $\phi_\mu = \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}\,\varphi_{1\mu} < \varphi_c$: only the target is in support.  The system can increase $v$ (rotating $\bx_\perp$ toward $\bxi^\mu_\perp$) while keeping $a = \phi_1$ fixed.  Since only $\bxi^1$ is in support and $\phi_1$ does not change, the energy is exactly constant along this rotation.

When $v$ reaches the entry value
\begin{equation}
v_{\mathrm{entry}} = \frac{\varphi_c - \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}\,\varphi_{1\mu}}{\sqrt{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2}}\,,
\label{eq:ventry}
\end{equation}
the spurious pattern enters support ($\phi_\mu = \varphi_c$).

\paragraph{Escape-path constraint.}
The rotation is possible only if $a^2 + v_{\mathrm{entry}}^2 \leq 1$ (the state must remain on the sphere).  This requires
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2 + \frac{(\varphi_c - \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}\,\varphi_{1\mu})^2}{1-\varphi_{1\mu}^2} \;\leq\; 1\,,
\label{eq:sphere_constraint}
\end{equation}
The quadratic in $\varphi_{1\mu}$ has two roots $\varphi_c\,\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} \pm \sqrt{(1-\varphi_c^2)(1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)}$, corresponding to three regimes.  Patterns with $\varphi_{1\mu}$ below the lower root cannot escape: $v_{\mathrm{entry}}$ is too large to fit on the sphere.  Patterns with $\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi_c/\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}$ are already in support at the retrieval state ($\phi_\mu = \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}\,\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi_c$ from~\eqref{eq:overlaps} at $v = 0$), so no barrier exists.  The upper root exceeds $\varphi_c/\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}$ and is therefore unphysical.  The escape-enabling patterns are those with overlap above the lower root:
\begin{equation}
\varphi_{\min}(\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}) = \varphi_c\,\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} - \sqrt{(1-\varphi_c^2)(1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)}\,.
\label{eq:phi_min}
\end{equation}
For $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$ and $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} = 0.90$: $\varphi_{\min} = 0.328$.  We denote by $K = n(\varphi_{\min})$ the number of escape-enabling patterns, i.e.\ the expected number of spurious patterns with $\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi_{\min}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:n_count}).  Table~\ref{tab:n_overlap} gives $K$ at representative $\alpha$ values, computed by numerical integration of the exact density~\eqref{eq:exact_density}.

The strong $\alpha$-dependence of $K$ reflects the competition between $M = e^{\alpha N}$ patterns and the tail decay of the exact density~\eqref{eq:exact_density}: $K \approx M \times \Pr(\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi_{\min}) \sim \exp\!\big(\alpha N + \tfrac{N}{2}\ln(1-\varphi_{\min}^2)\big)$.  At large $N$ (small $\alpha$), the distribution of $\varphi_{1\mu}$ concentrates near zero (variance $1/N$), so $\varphi_{\min} = 0.328$ falls deep in the tail and $K \ll 1$.  At small $N$ (large $\alpha$), the distribution is broader and $K$ grows rapidly.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_dist} summarizes the three overlap thresholds on the exact density for $N = 16$: the geometric cap intersection ($\varphi_{1\mu} > 2\varphi_c^2 - 1 = 0$, involving $\sim M/2$ patterns), the escape-path constraint ($\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi_{\min}$, restricting to $K$ patterns), and the most probable hop target at $\varphi_{1\mu} \approx \varphi_{1,\max}$.

The Gaussian and exact densities agree in the bulk ($\varphi \sim 1/\!\sqrt{N}$, where both concentrate) but diverge in the tail ($\varphi = O(1)$, visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:overlap_dist}).  This divergence does not diminish at large~$N$; it grows.  The extreme value $\varphi_{1,\max}$ sits at a fixed $\varphi = O(1)$, where the two densities decay at different exponential rates: $\exp(-N\varphi^2/2)$ for the Gaussian versus $\exp(\tfrac{N}{2}\ln(1-\varphi^2))$ for the exact.  Even a small difference in per-pattern tail probability is amplified exponentially by the $M = e^{\alpha N}$ independent draws from the distribution: the expected number of patterns above threshold, $n(\varphi^*) = M \times \Pr(\varphi_{1\mu} > \varphi^*)$, differs by a factor $\exp(cN)$ between the two densities.  This is why $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$ differs by a fixed amount (0.25 vs 0.347) that does not vanish as $N \to \infty$.

\paragraph{Stage 2: downhill slide ($\Delta E < 0$).}
Once both patterns are in support, the sum inside the logarithm in~\eqref{eq:lsr} increases and the energy decreases.  The system slides to the two-pattern centroid~\eqref{eq:centroid}.  The energy barrier along the full escape path is \emph{zero}: Stage~1 has $\Delta E = 0$ and Stage~2 has $\Delta E < 0$.

\paragraph{The entropic barrier.}
What prevents instantaneous escape is entropy, not energy.  The rotation in Stage~1 must align the $(N\!-\!1)$-dimensional perpendicular component $\bx_\perp$ with a specific direction ($\bxi^\mu_\perp$).  The perpendicular component lives on a sphere of radius $\sqrt{N(1-a^2)}$; increasing $v$ from $0$ to $v_{\mathrm{entry}}$ reduces the available perpendicular volume.  The free energy cost of this alignment is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta F}{T} = \frac{N-3}{2}\left[-\ln\!\left(1 - \frac{v_{\mathrm{entry}}^2}{1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2}\right)\right].
\label{eq:kramers_barrier}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Kramers escape and the tunneling analogy.}
The situation is mathematically identical to a particle in a potential well escaping over a barrier by thermal fluctuations (Kramers problem) or, equivalently, to radioactive alpha decay through a Coulomb barrier.  The variable $v$ diffuses in the entropic potential~\eqref{eq:kramers_barrier}, and each thermal fluctuation that reaches $v = v_{\mathrm{entry}}$ triggers an irreversible escape.  The attempt frequency is set by the relaxation time $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}$ of the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck diffusion in the perpendicular subspace, and the ``tunneling probability'' per attempt is $e^{-\Delta F/T}$.

For a single escape-enabling pattern, the escape time is
\begin{equation}
\tau_\mu \;=\; \tau_{\mathrm{rel}} \times \exp\!\left(\frac{\Delta F}{T}\right),
\label{eq:kramers_time}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}} \approx (1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)\,N^2 / (2.88\,T^2)$ is the OU relaxation time for the Metropolis random walk with step size $\sigma = 2.4\,T/\sqrt{N}$.  The escape is a Poisson process: the waiting time is exponentially distributed, and the probability of escaping within $t$ MC steps is
\begin{equation}
P(\text{escape by } t) = 1 - \exp(-t/\tau_\mu)\,.
\label{eq:escape_prob}
\end{equation}
Even when $\tau_\mu \gg T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ (the allocated MC time), a nonzero fraction $\approx T_{\mathrm{MC}}/\tau_\mu$ of trials escapes.  This is analogous to a radioactive sample: the half-life may be billions of years, but a measurable fraction of atoms decays on any timescale.

Patterns with lower $\varphi_{1\mu}$ have larger $v_{\mathrm{entry}}$ and exponentially higher barriers.  Each escape-enabling pattern runs an independent Poisson clock with rate $\lambda_\mu = \tau_\mu^{-1}$.  The total escape rate is $\lambda = \sum_\mu \lambda_\mu$, but since $\tau_\mu$ depends exponentially on $\Delta F_\mu/T$, the sum is dominated by the pattern with the smallest barrier, $\varphi_{1\mu} = \varphi_{1,\max}$:
\begin{equation}
P(\text{escape by } t) = 1 - \exp(-\lambda\, t) \;\approx\; 1 - \exp(-t/\tau_{\mathrm{eff}})\,,
\qquad \tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \tau_{\mathrm{rel}} \times \exp\!\left(\frac{\Delta F}{T}\bigg|_{\varphi_{1,\max}}\right).
\label{eq:escape_prob_K}
\end{equation}

At $\alpha = 0.20$ ($\varphi_{1,\max} \approx 0.57$, $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}} = 0.90$, $N = 50$): $v_{\mathrm{entry}} = 0.232$, $\Delta F/T \approx 9.0$.  The targeted escape-time measurements in Section~\ref{ssec:escape_times} calibrate the effective escape time as $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ with $c \approx 0.38$.

\subsection{Two-pattern thermal equilibrium}
\label{ssec:retrieval}

The geometric centroid~\eqref{eq:centroid} is the $T = 0$ energy minimum.  At finite temperature, the system thermalizes in the two-pattern basin, balancing the energy $u_2(\phi) = -b^{-1}\ln[2(1-b+b\phi)]$ against the entropy of the perpendicular subspace, $s_2(\phi, \varphi_{1\mu}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1 - 2\phi^2/(1+\varphi_{1\mu}))$.  Setting $\partial(u_2 - T\,s_2)/\partial\phi = 0$ gives the two-pattern self-consistency equation:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{1-b+b\,\phi} = \frac{2\,T\,\phi}{(1+\varphi_{1\mu}) - 2\phi^2}\,.
\label{eq:two_pat_sc}
\end{equation}
The equilibrium centroid overlap $\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}(\varphi_{1\mu}, T)$ depends on both the inter-pattern overlap and the temperature.

The system escapes to the pattern with the largest overlap $\varphi_{1\mu} \approx \varphi_{1,\max}$ (smallest barrier).  Since $\varphi_{1,\max}$ varies slowly with $\alpha$ ($0.51$--$0.67$ for $\alpha = 0.15$--$0.30$) and $\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}$ is a slowly varying function of $\varphi_{1\mu}$, the centroid peak position is approximately $\alpha$-independent.  At $T = 0.325$: $\varphi_{\mathrm{cen}} \approx 0.82$--$0.84$ across this range, consistent with the observed peak at $\varphi \approx 0.83$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf}).

\subsection{Centroid hopping}
\label{ssec:hopping_theory}

After the system reaches the centroid $(\bxi^1, \bxi^\mu)$, it can escape again by the same mechanism: pattern $\bxi^\mu$ has its own high-overlap neighbors, and one of them, $\bxi^\nu$, can be recruited via a perpendicular rotation in the remaining $(N\!-\!2)$-dimensional subspace.  This time, it is $\bxi^1$ that is pushed out of support while $\bxi^\nu$ enters.  The result is a new two-pattern centroid $(\bxi^\mu, \bxi^\nu)$ at comparable energy:
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\bxi^1}_{\phi_1 = 0.90}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{1st hop}}\;
\underbrace{(\bxi^1,\,\bxi^\mu)}_{\phi_1 \approx 0.83}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{2nd hop}}\;
\underbrace{(\bxi^\mu,\,\bxi^\nu)}_{\phi_1 \approx 0.48}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{3rd hop}}\;
\underbrace{(\bxi^\nu,\,\bxi^\rho)}_{\phi_1 \approx 0.1\text{--}0.2}
\;\to\; \cdots
\label{eq:hopping_chain}
\end{equation}

After the second hop, the target is no longer in support ($\phi_1 < \varphi_c$).  Its residual overlap with the active spurious pair is
\begin{equation}
\phi_1 = \frac{\varphi_{1\mu} + \varphi_{1\nu}}{\sqrt{2(1+\varphi_{\mu\nu})}}\,.
\label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
The overlap $\varphi_{1\nu}$ between the target and the newly recruited pattern $\bxi^\nu$ is not independent of $\varphi_{1\mu}$: since $\bxi^\nu$ was selected for having high overlap with $\bxi^\mu$, decomposing $\bxi^\nu$ along $\bxi^\mu$ and perpendicular gives
\begin{equation}
E[\varphi_{1\nu} \mid \varphi_{\mu\nu}] = \varphi_{1\mu}\,\varphi_{\mu\nu}\,.
\label{eq:conditional}
\end{equation}
For $\varphi_{1\mu} \approx \varphi_{\mu\nu} \approx 0.57$ (the most-overlapping neighbors at $\alpha = 0.20$): $\varphi_{1\nu} \approx 0.33$.  Substituting into~\eqref{eq:residual}: $\phi_1 \approx (0.57 + 0.33)/\sqrt{2 \times 1.57} \approx 0.51$, consistent with the observed peak near $\varphi \approx 0.48$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf}).

The dynamics is a random walk on the pattern overlap graph, where each step swaps one member of the active pair for a neighbor of the other.  The two-pattern centroid energy is approximately constant along this walk; only the target's overlap decreases.

\paragraph{No true paramagnetic state.}
In the LSR model, the energy~\eqref{eq:lsr} diverges ($\to +\infty$) when no pattern is in support, because the argument of the logarithm vanishes.  The system is therefore \emph{always} at some centroid.  This can be verified by measuring the maximum alignment with any non-target pattern, $\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} = \max_{\mu \neq 1} \phi_\mu$: even trials with $\phi_1 \approx 0$ show $\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} \approx 0.82$, confirming that the system sits at a two-pattern centroid of distant patterns rather than in a paramagnetic state.

\subsection{Predicted escape times and probe-point selection}
\label{ssec:escape_predictions}

The MC simulations in Section~\ref{ssec:escape_times} show that the effective escape time is well described by
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\,\exp\!\left(c\,\frac{\Delta F}{T}\right),
\label{eq:tau_eff}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta F/T$ is evaluated at $\varphi_{1\mu} = \varphi_{1,\max}$ (the pattern with the lowest barrier), $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}} = (1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)N^2/(2.88\,T^2)$ is the OU diffusion time, the factor $N$ accounts for geometric dilution (the MC walk distributes its displacement over $N-1$ tangent directions), and $c \approx 0.38$ is calibrated from escape-time measurements.  Since the second hop has a comparable barrier (Section~\ref{ssec:escape}), the time to observe $k$ hops is approximately $k\,\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

The barrier $\Delta F/T$ depends weakly on $T$ (it is set primarily by the geometry through $v_{\mathrm{entry}}$ and $\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}$), but the escape time varies over many orders of magnitude due to the exponential.

\section{Monte Carlo simulations}
\label{sec:mc}

\subsection{Simulation protocol}
\label{ssec:protocol}

We simulate the Boltzmann distribution $\propto \exp(-E(\bx)/T)$ for the LSR energy~\eqref{eq:lsr} using Metropolis--Hastings Monte Carlo on $S^{N-1}(\!\sqrt{N})$.  At each step, a candidate state is proposed as $\bx' = \bx + \sigma\boldsymbol{\eta}$, $\boldsymbol{\eta} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_N)$, projected onto the sphere ($\bx' \leftarrow \sqrt{N}\,\bx'/\|\bx'\|$), and accepted with probability $\min(1, e^{-\Delta E/T})$.  The step size $\sigma = 2.4\,T/\sqrt{N}$ is adapted to the temperature and dimension.  Proposals falling outside the support of all patterns ($E = +\infty$) are automatically rejected.

Two types of simulations are reported.

\paragraph{Bulk survey.}  A grid of $110$ values of $\alpha \in [0.005, 0.550]$ and $40$ temperature values is swept, with $M = 20\,000$ patterns and $N = \lfloor\ln M/\alpha\rfloor$ ($N = 12$--$69$, depending on $\alpha$).  For each of $n_{\mathrm{dis}} = 256$ independent disorder samples, two replicas (A, B) are initialized near the target $\bxi^1$ with $\phi_{\mathrm{init}} \sim \mathrm{U}(0.75, 1.0)$ and evolved independently.  Each replica undergoes $N_{\mathrm{eq}} = 2^{15}$ equilibration steps followed by $N_{\mathrm{samp}} = 2^{13}$ sampling steps ($T_{\mathrm{MC}} = N_{\mathrm{eq}} + N_{\mathrm{samp}} \approx 4 \times 10^4$ steps total).  The per-trial observables are the time-averaged target alignment $\varphi_A$, $\varphi_B$, the Edwards--Anderson overlap $q_{\mathrm{EA}} = (1/N_{\mathrm{samp}})\sum_t \bx_A(t)\!\cdot\!\bx_B(t)/N$, and the maximum spurious overlap $\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} = (1/N_{\mathrm{samp}})\sum_t \max_{\mu\neq 1}\phi_\mu(\bx_A(t))$.

\paragraph{Targeted escape-time measurements.}  At $16$ selected $(\alpha, T)$ points ($\alpha \in \{0.18, 0.20\}$, $T \in \{0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50, 0.80\}$), the full trajectory $\varphi(t)$ is recorded every $64$ MC steps over $2^{18} \approx 2.6 \times 10^5$ total steps, with $n_{\mathrm{dis}} = 2000$ independent disorder samples per point.  The run length is $6.4$ times longer than the bulk survey, and the larger $n_{\mathrm{dis}}$ reduces the statistical uncertainty of the escape fraction.  These simulations are performed on GPU with half-precision arithmetic and batched matrix operations to accommodate $2000$ independent pattern sets simultaneously.

\subsection{Phase diagram}
\label{ssec:phase_diagram}

Figure~\ref{fig:phase_diagram} connects the Kramers escape theory to the observed phase diagram.

The left panel validates the exponential barrier scaling.  The Poisson-inverted escape time $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}$~\eqref{eq:tau_poisson}, measured from the targeted simulations ($n_{\mathrm{dis}} = 2000$, $T_{\mathrm{MC}} = 2^{18}$), is divided by $N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}$, where $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}} = (1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)N^2/(2.88\,T^2)$ is the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck diffusion time.  The factor of $N$ accounts for the geometric dilution: the MC random walk explores $N-1$ tangent directions on the sphere uniformly, so the escape coordinate $v$ receives only $\sim 1/N$ of the effective diffusion.  For $\alpha = 0.20$ ($N = 49$, red squares), $\ln(\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}/(N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}))$ is linear in $\Delta F/T$ with slope $c = 0.37$, $R^2 = 0.95$, and intercept $\approx 0$.  The $\alpha = 0.18$ ($N = 55$, blue circles) and $\alpha = 0.22$ ($N = 45$, green diamonds) points are shown for comparison; the latter sit close to the $\alpha = 0.20$ fit.

The right panel shows the disorder-averaged alignment $\avg{\varphi}(\alpha, T)$ from the bulk survey ($M = 20\,000$, $n_{\mathrm{dis}} = 256$, $T_{\mathrm{MC}} \approx 4 \times 10^4$ steps).  Four boundaries are overlaid.  The green curve is the empirical kinetic boundary $P_{\mathrm{esc}} = 0.5$.  A trial is classified as escaped if its time-averaged alignment falls below $\varphi_{\mathrm{thresh}}(T) = (\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}(T) + \varphi_{\mathrm{cen}}(T))/2$, the midpoint between the single-pattern retrieval equilibrium (ibid., Eq.~37) and the two-pattern centroid equilibrium~\eqref{eq:two_pat_sc}.  This $T$-dependent threshold tracks both peaks as they shift with temperature, maintaining a clean separation between retrieval and escaped populations.  The red curve is the Kramers prediction $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ from the calibrated formula~\eqref{eq:kramers_fit}.  The cyan and black curves are the noise-floor thresholds $\alpha_c(T)$ from the Gaussian and exact alignment distributions, respectively.

The centroid escape theory of Section~\ref{sec:theory} reinterprets the right panel:
\begin{itemize}
\item The ``retrieval'' region (blue) is \emph{metastable}: the Kramers escape time exceeds $T_{\mathrm{MC}}$, so the system has not yet escaped within the allocated MC time.  With longer runs, $\avg{\varphi}$ would decrease as more trials escape to centroids.
\item The ``non-retrieval'' region (red) is \emph{not paramagnetic}: $\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} \approx 0.82$ throughout, confirming that the system sits at a two-pattern centroid (Section~\ref{ssec:hopping_theory}).  A true paramagnetic state ($\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} \to 0$) does not exist in LSR because the energy diverges when no pattern is in support.
\item The boundary is a \emph{kinetic crossover}, not a thermodynamic phase transition: it marks where $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx T_{\mathrm{MC}}$, not where a free-energy minimum disappears.  Moving the boundary requires only changing the simulation time.
\end{itemize}

The overlap distribution of Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_dist} provides the key theoretical input: it determines $\varphi_{1,\max}(\alpha)$ and $K(\alpha)$, which set the barrier height~\eqref{eq:kramers_barrier} and hence $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}$ at each point of the $(\alpha, T)$ plane.

\paragraph{Residual overlap at the spurious centroid.}

\subsection{Escape-time measurements}
\label{ssec:escape_times}

The targeted simulations measure the escape fraction $P_{\mathrm{esc}}$ (fraction of trials with $\varphi < \varphi_{\mathrm{thresh}}(T)$, defined above) at each $(\alpha, T)$ point.  For a Kramers process with exponentially distributed escape times, $P_{\mathrm{esc}} = 1 - \exp(-T_{\mathrm{MC}}/\tau_{\mathrm{eff}})$, which gives the Poisson-inverted escape time
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = -\frac{T_{\mathrm{MC}}}{\ln(1 - P_{\mathrm{esc}})}\,.
\label{eq:tau_poisson}
\end{equation}
The measured $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}$ spans three decades, from $\sim 10^5$ MC steps ($\alpha = 0.20$, $T = 0.80$, most trials escape) to $\sim 10^{7.7}$ ($\alpha = 0.20$, $T = 0.10$, only $0.5\%$ escape within $2^{18}$ steps).

Figure~\ref{fig:phase_diagram} (left) plots $\ln(\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}/(N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}))$ against $\Delta F/T$, where $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}} = (1-\phi_{\mathrm{eq}}^2)N^2/(2.88\,T^2)$.  The factor $N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}$ absorbs the known scaling: $\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}$ is the OU diffusion time for the perpendicular coordinate $v$, and $N$ accounts for the geometric dilution (the MC random walk distributes its displacement over $N-1$ tangent directions, so $v$ receives $\sim 1/N$ of the effective diffusion).  The linear trend for $\alpha = 0.20$ gives
\begin{equation}
\ln\!\left(\frac{\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}}{N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}}\right) \;\approx\; 0.38 \times \frac{\Delta F}{T}\,,
\qquad R^2 = 0.95\,,
\label{eq:kramers_fit}
\end{equation}
with intercept $\approx 0$, confirming that the escape time scales as $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ with $c \approx 0.38$ and no additional free parameter.  The slope $c \approx 0.38$ means the effective barrier is $\approx 37\%$ of the formula~\eqref{eq:kramers_barrier}.  Two effects contribute: (i)~at thermal equilibrium the system starts at $v \sim T/\sqrt{N}$, not $v = 0$, shortening the effective distance to $v_{\mathrm{entry}}$; (ii)~the one-dimensional projection neglects favorable fluctuations in the remaining $N-2$ coordinates.

The calibrated formula $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ produces the red contour $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ in Figure~\ref{fig:phase_diagram} (right).  The contour is shifted to the left of the empirical boundary (green) because the calibration is based on $\alpha = 0.20$ only; the remaining probe points at $\alpha = 0.22$ and $0.24$ will tighten the extrapolation.  The qualitative agreement in shape confirms that the apparent ``phase boundary'' is the kinetic locus $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}}(\alpha, T) \approx T_{\mathrm{MC}}$.

\paragraph{Practical relevance.}
The escape time $\tau \sim \exp(cN)$ grows exponentially with the system dimension, because the barrier $\Delta F/T \propto N$.  At $N = 50$ and $\alpha = 0.20$: $\tau \sim 10^6$ MC steps.  At $N = 100$, the barrier roughly doubles, giving $\tau \sim 10^{12}$.  For any practical retrieval application with $N \gg 50$, the escape time far exceeds any realistic query time.  The retrieval state, though formally metastable, is effectively stable.

\section{Discussion and summary}
\label{sec:discussion}

The thermodynamic theory of Dense Associative Memory predicts a sharp retrieval phase transition at $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}$: perfect retrieval below, complete failure above.  For the LSR kernel with compact support, this picture is incomplete.  The Monte Carlo overlap distributions reveal a richer landscape \emph{within} the retrieval region, with secondary peaks at $\varphi \approx 0.83$ and $0.48$ that grow with $\alpha$ and $T$.

This paper identifies these peaks as two-pattern centroids and explains their origin through a Kramers escape mechanism.  The key results are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Centroid escape.}  The compact support of the LSR kernel creates a zero-energy escape path: a perpendicular rotation that brings a spurious pattern into support without changing the target alignment.  The only cost is entropic, giving a barrier $\Delta F/T \propto N$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:kramers_barrier}).  The system escapes via thermal fluctuations, with an exponentially distributed waiting time validated by MC measurements ($R^2 = 0.95$, Fig.~\ref{fig:phase_diagram} left).

\item \textbf{Centroid hopping.}  After the first escape, the system performs a random walk on the pattern overlap graph~\eqref{eq:hopping_chain}, hopping between two-pattern centroids at comparable energy.  No true paramagnetic state exists: $\phi_{\max}^{\mathrm{other}} \approx 0.82$ even for trials with $\varphi \approx 0$ (Table~\ref{tab:phimax}).

\item \textbf{Kinetic, not thermodynamic boundary.}  The apparent phase boundary at $\alpha \approx 0.25$ is the kinetic locus $\tau_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:phase_diagram} right), not a thermodynamic singularity.  It shifts with the simulation time.

\item \textbf{Practical stability.}  The escape time $\tau \sim N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ with $\Delta F/T \propto N$ grows exponentially with $N$.  At $N = 50$ and $\alpha = 0.20$: $\tau \sim 10^6$ MC steps.  At $N = 100$: $\tau \sim 10^{12}$.  For practical retrieval ($N \gg 50$), the metastable retrieval state is effectively permanent.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Finite-$N$ origin of the apparent Gaussian boundary.}
The MC simulations use $N \approx 40$--$50$, where the Kramers escape time $\tau \sim N\,\tau_{\mathrm{rel}}\exp(c\,\Delta F/T)$ is comparable to $T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ near $\alpha \approx 0.25$.  This coincidence with the Gaussian noise-floor threshold $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}} = \varphi_c^2/2 = 0.25$ is accidental: it depends on $T_{\mathrm{MC}}$ and the system sizes accessible to the simulation.  At larger $N$, the barrier $\Delta F/T \propto N$ grows and the escape becomes unobservable; the kinetic boundary shifts rightward and eventually reaches the exact-density threshold $\alpha_{\mathrm{th}}^{\mathrm{exact}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1-\varphi_c^2) \approx 0.347$ (the black curve in Fig.~\ref{fig:phase_diagram}, right).  At this loading, a spurious pattern enters support at the retrieval state ($\phi_\mu = \phi_{\mathrm{eq}}\,\varphi_{1,\max} > \varphi_c$) and the barrier vanishes: retrieval is lost without any thermal activation.  The Gaussian approximation does not become ``more correct'' at finite~$N$; rather, the kinetic crossover happens to pass through $\alpha \approx 0.25$ at the system sizes used in the simulations.

\paragraph{Comparison with classical Hopfield models.}
The centroids observed here are analogous to the mixture states of the classical Hopfield network~\citep{amit1987statistical}, where correlated superpositions of stored patterns form metastable fixed points.  There are two key differences.  First, in the Hopfield model, mixture states arise from the symmetry of the Hebbian interaction and exist as true fixed points of the zero-temperature dynamics; here, the centroids are thermal equilibria stabilized by entropy, not energy.  Second, in the Hopfield model, the paramagnetic state ($m = 0$) is a legitimate phase; in LSR, the energy diverges when no pattern is in support, so the system is always at some centroid.

\paragraph{Open questions.}
(i)~The barrier scaling factor $c \approx 0.38$ is empirical; a derivation from the multi-dimensional Kramers (Langer) theory would make it parameter-free.  (ii)~The analysis assumes $b = 2+\sqrt{2}$; for larger $b$ (narrower support caps), the cluster criterion~\eqref{eq:cluster} changes and multi-pattern centroids may become relevant.  (iii)~The connection to the LSE kernel, where the escape mechanism differs (no compact support, no zero-energy path), remains to be explored.
